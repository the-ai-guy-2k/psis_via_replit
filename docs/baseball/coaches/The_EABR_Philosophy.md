# The EABR Philosophy

Why every at-bat receives an evaluation — and what that evaluation means.

---

## What EABR Stands For

**E**nd of **A**t-**B**at **R**esult.

When the plate appearance ends, the pitcher and batter have produced a result. EABR asks one coaching question:

> **Who won this at-bat — the pitcher or the hitter?**

The answer is expressed in **good units** (pitcher credit) and **bad units** (pitcher debit).

---

## Why Every At-Bat Receives an Evaluation

### Baseball is sequential

An inning is not one event. It is a chain of plate appearances. A pitcher can dominate four hitters and unravel on the fifth. Season stats average that story away.

### Memory is unreliable

Coaches remember the homer. They forget the three quality outs before it. At-bat logging protects against **recency bias**.

### Development requires feedback loops

"You threw well" is not enough. "You won seven of ten at-bats, stranded four runners, and your sequences with the slider were positive" is actionable.

### Fairness requires consistency

If one coach calls a flyout "lucky" and another calls it "well executed," development conversations break down. EABR standardizes the **classification**, not the emotion.

---

## Why Each Event Has Different Value

EABR uses a **base rule** plus **context adjustments**:

| Layer | Rule | Philosophy |
|-------|------|------------|
| **Base** | Every defensive outcome = 1 good unit; every offensive outcome = 1 bad unit | The at-bat itself has a winner |
| **Damage** | Each RBI on an offensive at-bat adds 1 additional bad unit | Runs matter more than traffic alone |
| **Stranding** | Each runner left on base when the inning ends adds 1 good unit | Getting out of the inning without further scoring is success |

A ground-ball double play and a strikeout both earn **one** good unit at the base layer — but the double play also records two outs, advancing the inning faster. Damage and LOB capture what flat counting would miss.

**Important:** Hit type (single vs home run) does not multiply the base bad unit. A home run is still one offensive event — but RBI units capture the extra damage when runners score.

---

## Why Process Matters

Good coaches praise process even when results hurt:

- Competitive sliders that miss the bat
- Weak contact on the ground
- Strikeouts in leverage spots

EABR classifies **outcomes**, not pitch quality within the at-bat. That is intentional: outcome classification is coachable and loggable in real time. Pitch-level grades belong in bullpen work and video — but the at-bat result still answers whether the plan worked.

Process and outcome stay linked through **pitch sequences** (logged separately) and **session trends** over time.

---

## Why Context Matters

The same pitch type can be brilliant or catastrophic depending on situation. EABR's context layer focuses on what can be derived from **base-state logic**:

| Context | How EABR reflects it |
|---------|---------------------|
| **Runners on base** | Walks can force runs; hits drive in runners → RBI bad units |
| **Inning completion** | Runners stranded → LOB good units on the completing out |
| **Outs recorded** | Double and triple plays end innings faster (tracked as outs, not extra good units) |

EABR does not yet model count, hitter handedness, or leverage index. Those are known limitations — not philosophical rejections.

---

## Why Innings Matter

Innings are the natural boundary of a pitcher's defensive half-inning:

- **Delta per inning** shows whether the pitcher won or lost that frame
- **LOB at inning end** rewards escaping traffic
- **Three outs** closes the chapter before the next begins

Session summaries roll innings together — but the inning remains the **middle layer** between single at-bats and full outings.

---

## Why Consistency Matters

EABR only works when staff agree:

| Defense (good) | Offense (bad) |
|--------------|---------------|
| Strikeout | Hit (single, double, triple, home run) |
| Fly out | Walk |
| Ground out (including double/triple play) | |

Fly-out location (infield vs outfield) is recorded for coaching notes but does not change the unit score. Play result on ground outs (single vs double play) changes **outs**, not the base good unit.

Consistency enables:

- Comparable sessions across pitchers
- Honest staff meetings
- Trends that mean something

---

## The Summary Metrics

| Metric | Formula | Coaching read |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| **Good units** | Sum of pitcher-favorable credits | Volume of winning at-bats (+ LOB) |
| **Bad units** | Sum of pitcher-unfavorable debits (+ RBI) | Volume and cost of losing at-bats |
| **Delta** | Good − Bad | Did the pitcher win the sample? |
| **EABR Fraction** | Good ÷ Bad | Efficiency — higher is better |

Positive delta: pitcher-favorable sample.  
Fraction above 1.0: more good units than bad.

---

## EABR Is Not a Moral Judgment

A "bad unit" does not mean the pitcher threw badly. It means the **hitter won the at-bat** — sometimes despite quality pitches. Coaches still praise execution when EABR records a bad unit.

EABR describes **what happened**, not **who to blame**.
