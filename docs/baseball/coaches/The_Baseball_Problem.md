# The Baseball Problem

What pitching coaches struggle to evaluate — and why traditional statistics fall short.

---

## The Real Job of a Pitching Coach

A pitching coach is not paid to memorize ERA. A pitching coach is paid to help pitchers:

- Execute a plan against each hitter
- Repeat good processes under pressure
- Learn from outings without being fooled by noise
- Develop over weeks and months, not just survive today's box score

That job requires **granular memory**: what happened in each plate appearance, in what context, and whether the pitcher won or lost that battle.

Traditional season statistics compress thousands of pitches into a handful of numbers. Useful — but incomplete.

---

## Why ERA Is Not Enough

**ERA (Earned Run Average)** answers: *How many earned runs did this pitcher allow per nine innings?*

### What ERA measures well

- Bottom-line run prevention over time
- Comparison across pitchers in similar environments
- A language scouts and front offices understand

### What ERA misses

| Gap | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Process vs outcome** | Three hard lineouts and a bloop triple — ERA remembers the run |
| **Defense and luck** | Error-extended innings, weak contact that falls |
| **At-bat granularity** | No record of which plate appearances were won |
| **Sequencing** | ERA does not know you set up the slider with two fastballs |
| **Small samples** | One bad inning dominates a bullpen appearance |
| **Context** | Same ERA from dominant strikeouts vs. constant traffic |

ERA tells you **what scored**. It does not tell you **who won each fight**.

---

## Why WHIP Is Not Enough

**WHIP (Walks + Hits per Inning Pitched)** answers: *How many baserunners per inning?*

### What WHIP measures well

- Traffic allowed — a simple pressure gauge
- Walk control as part of the equation

### What WHIP misses

| Gap | Example |
|-----|---------|
| **Quality of contact** | A weak groundout and a ringing double count the same (hit) |
| **Strikeouts** | A 1-2-3 strikeout inning and a three-run homer inning can look similar if hits/walks align |
| **Extra-base damage** | WHIP treats all hits equally |
| **Sequencing and execution** | No pitch-by-pitch story |
| **Inning-level process** | WHIP is an average, not a battle log |

WHIP tells you **how crowded the bases were**. It does not tell you **whether the pitcher executed the plan**.

---

## Why Wins Are Not Enough

**Wins** answer: *Did the pitcher's team win when he was the pitcher of record?*

Wins are a **team outcome**, not a pitching process metric.

| Problem | Reality |
|---------|---------|
| Run support | A pitcher can dominate and lose 2–1 |
| Bullpen | Starter leaves with a lead; reliever blows it |
| Offense | No run support masks strong outings |
| Role | Relievers rarely get wins despite high leverage |

No serious pitching development program should rank pitchers by wins alone.

---

## What Else Coaches Reach For

| Stat | Strength | Limitation |
|------|----------|------------|
| **Strikeouts** | Measures miss ability | Ignores contact management |
| **Ground ball %** | Rewards certain approaches | Does not measure sequencing or weak contact |
| **FIP** | Strips defense/luck from run prevention | Still inning-aggregated; no at-bat story |
| **Quality Starts** | Rewards length + run limit | Binary; ignores how those runs happened |
| **Pitch counts** | Workload management | Says nothing about quality |

Each answers a different question. None replaces the coach's question: **"Did we win that at-bat?"**

---

## The Missing Information

Coaches routinely need answers traditional stats do not provide:

1. **At-bat outcomes** — Win, lose, or neutralize each plate appearance
2. **Inning narrative** — How did the pitcher navigate traffic?
3. **Sequence memory** — What pitches preceded success or failure?
4. **Consistency** — Is today's outing an outlier or a trend?
5. **Development signal** — Is the process improving even when results lag?

Spreadsheets, notebooks, and memory fill this gap today. They are inconsistent, hard to aggregate, and rarely shared across a staff.

---

## What Coaches Should Really Be Evaluating

| Evaluate | Because |
|----------|---------|
| **Each plate appearance** | Baseball is decided one hitter at a time |
| **Process before results** | Good pitches get hit; bad pitches sometimes work |
| **Context** | Runners on base change the cost of mistakes |
| **Inning management** | Stranding runners is a pitching success |
| **Patterns over time** | One outing is a chapter, not the book |
| **Sequencing** | Execution includes setup, not just velocity |

PSIS was created to make these evaluations **repeatable and discussable** — not to replace the coach in the dugout.

---

## The Problem, Stated Simply

> **Pitching coaches need at-bat-level truth. Traditional statistics offer season-level summaries. The gap between them is where development lives — and where most tools do not go.**
