# Why I Built PSIS

*A personal note — the kind I'd give you if we were sitting in the bleachers before first pitch.*

---

## The Question That Wouldn't Leave Me Alone

After enough games — high school, summer ball, the ones you watch on TV — you start asking the same thing:

> **"Did that pitcher really pitch badly tonight, or did the box score just make it look that way?"**

Not as a statistics problem. As a **baseball** problem.

You have seen it a hundred times. A kid throws his heart out for five innings. He wins more battles than he loses. Then one bloop, one booted grounder, one hitter who refuses to chase — and the line in the paper reads like a disaster.

The people in the stands who watched every pitch know better. The line in the agate type does not.

I built PSIS because I wanted **what the attentive fan already knows** to be written down the same way every time — at-bat by at-bat — so coaches and pitchers could talk about the real game, not just the summary.

---

## What I Was Watching

I kept noticing the same patterns:

| What I saw | What the box score said |
|------------|-------------------------|
| Pitcher battles out of a bases-loaded jam | "Gave up a run" |
| Weak contact all night, three bleeders fall | "Rough outing" |
| Line drive caught at the wall | Same as a pop-up — just "out" |
| Pitcher sets up the slider with two fastballs, gets the whiff | Nothing — the K is there, the setup is not |

Old scouts and old coaches always talked this way. *"He pitched better than his line."* *"That inning could have been worse."* *"Remember the sequence on that strikeout."*

Nobody was **keeping** that story in one place.

---

## Traditional Stats Did Not Fail Me — They Just Ended Too Soon

I want to be clear: I did not build PSIS because I hate ERA.

ERA matters. Wins matter on the right night. WHIP tells you something true about traffic. Strikeouts have meant the same thing since Christy Mathewson.

Traditional statistics **summarize**. They were never meant to be the entire conversation after a bullpen session or a Tuesday night start.

PSIS starts where the box score **stops** — at the plate appearance. One hitter. One result. One chapter in the outing.

That is not anti-tradition. That is **how people who love baseball have always watched the game** — one at-bat at a time — before anyone put a computer in the press box.

---

## The Name Says What I Care About

**Pitch Sequence Intelligence** — not because a machine is smart, but because **sequencing is intelligence on the mound**.

Fastball in, slider away. Change the eye level. Know what you threw two pitches ago. That is baseball craft. It is older than any formula.

PSIS remembers the sequence and the result together. So when someone asks *"what worked?"* you have an answer that is not just a feeling.

---

## Who This Is For

PSIS is a coaching tool at heart. But the **philosophy** is for anyone who has ever argued about a pitcher in a diner after a game:

- The uncle who kept a scorebook in the third row
- The former player who trusts his eyes
- The fan who does not need launch angle to know a hanging curve when he sees one

If that is you — this documentation is our side of that argument. Respectfully.

---

## What I Am Not Claiming

I am not claiming PSIS replaces watching the game.  
I am not claiming every old-school instinct was wrong.  
I am not claiming you need software to love baseball.

I am claiming that **the stories we already tell about pitching deserve to be captured** — consistently, honestly, at-bat by at-bat — so development and discussion can catch up to what the best fans and coaches already see.

That is why I built it.
