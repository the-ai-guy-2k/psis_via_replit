# How PSIS Fits Into Baseball

An honest placement — no revolution promised.

---

## What PSIS Is

A way to **remember pitching outings at-bat by at-bat** — including pitch sequences and whether the pitcher or hitter won each confrontation, with honest weight for damage and stranded runners.

That is the whole idea.

---

## What PSIS Is Not Trying to Replace

| Institution | PSIS stance |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Scouts** | Eyes and experience still find players |
| **Coaches** | Development still happens in bullpens and conversations |
| **Scorebooks** | The uncle in section 12 was never wrong to keep score by hand |
| **ERA, WHIP, wins** | Season summaries still matter |
| **Watching the game** | No substitute for being there |

PSIS is a **lens** — one more way to talk about what you already saw.

---

## Where It Sits in the Baseball World

```
You watch the game          (primary — always)
        ↓
You form opinions           (instinct, memory, argument)
        ↓
Box score summarizes        (tradition — still valid)
        ↓
At-bat log adds detail      (PSIS — optional depth)
        ↓
Coach and pitcher act       (development — the real point)
```

Nothing in that chain **replaces** the top step.

---

## Who Uses It Today

Primarily **pitching coaches and staffs** logging sessions for development — not broadcasters replacing ERA on screen, not fans required to learn new vocabulary to enjoy a Sunday game.

If you never touch PSIS, baseball is still baseball.

If your team's coach uses it, you might hear **"good units"** or **"delta"** — plain language for **won more at-bats than lost** and **net score for the outing**. You can ignore the terms and still follow the story.

---

## Another Lens — Not a New Religion

Think of it like replay:

- Replay did not replace umpires
- Replay added **clarity on close plays**

PSIS does not replace judgment. It adds **clarity on outings** when two lines look alike and only the people who watched know the difference.

---

## For the Skeptical Fan

You do not have to adopt PSIS to respect it.

You only have to admit:

1. At-bats matter
2. Sequences matter
3. The box score sometimes lies by omission

PSIS lives in that admission. **So do most old-school baseball people** — they just never had a shared notebook.
