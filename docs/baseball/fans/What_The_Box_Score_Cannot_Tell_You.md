# What the Box Score Cannot Tell You

The box score is a masterpiece of compression. That is also its limitation.

---

## What the Box Score Does Brilliantly

In thirty seconds you learn:

- How many innings the pitcher went
- How many runs and earned runs
- How many hits and walks
- How many strikeouts

For a century, that was enough to argue about pitching over breakfast. **It still is** — for the big picture.

But the box score is a **summary**. Summaries leave things out.

---

## Situation One: He Dominated — Then One Bloop Changed the Inning

**What you saw:** Six punchouts. Weak contact. Then a flare over the shortstop's head — one run — and the inning feels different than it was.

**What the line says:** "1 ER, 6 IP" — maybe fine, maybe not. It does not say **five of six at-bats were wins**.

The attentive fan knows the truth. The newspaper does not have room.

---

## Situation Two: Defense Makes an Error

**What you saw:** Ground ball, should be an inning-ender. Booted. Runner on. Pressure changes. Next hitter capitalizes.

**What the line says:** Earned run average may forgive the run — or may not, depending on the scorer. Either way, **the pitcher's battle record that inning** is buried.

Fans argue about earned vs unearned. Coaches argue about **what pitch led to the grounder**. Both conversations matter. The box score only hosts one.

---

## Situation Three: A Hard-Hit Ball Is Caught

**What you saw:** Rocket to the gap. Center fielder runs it down. You exhale.

**What the line says:** "F8" — fly out. Same notation as a lazy pop-up.

Your gut knows the difference. The agate type does not.

---

## Situation Four: A Weak Dribbler Becomes a Hit

**What you saw:** Chopper three feet from home plate. Infielder has no play. Hit.

**What the line says:** "H" — same letter as a line drive.

**What you felt:** Unlucky. And maybe it was. The box score does not carry **how** the hit happened — only that it did.

---

## Situation Five: He Escaped Bases Loaded

**What you saw:** Walk, single, walk — bases loaded, one out. Strikeout. Ground ball to second — double play. Inning over. Crowd roars.

**What the line says:** Maybe one run, maybe more — it does not highlight **that he stranded three**.

Old-school fans call that **competing**. PSIS calls that worth remembering — at-bat by at-bat, including the runners left when the third out is recorded.

---

## Why Every Outing Deserves More Than One Line

You would not summarize a novel in one sentence. A pitching outing is a **story** — innings, hitters, sequences, jams, escapes.

The box score is the **last page** of the cliff notes.

PSIS is not trying to kill the box score. It is trying to give the people who care — coaches, pitchers, fans who pay attention — a way to **keep the chapters**, not just the ending.

---

## The Fan's Test

Ask yourself after the next game you watch:

> "Could I reconstruct **who won each at-bat** from the line score alone?"

If the answer is no — that is not a failure of baseball. That is a limitation of compression.

And that is okay — **as long as we admit it** when we judge a young pitcher or debate a veteran's night.
