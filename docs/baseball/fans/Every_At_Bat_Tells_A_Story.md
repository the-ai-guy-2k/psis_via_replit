# Every At-Bat Tells a Story

Baseball on television moves hitter to hitter. Baseball in the stands moves the same way. The game has always been **one confrontation at a time**.

---

## A Game Is a Collection of Battles

Nine innings sounds big. But watch closely and you see:

```
Hitter steps in → Pitcher competes → Outcome → Next hitter
```

Over and over. That is the game. Not ERA. Not WHIP. **This hitter, right now.**

PSIS begins at the at-bat because **that is where baseball begins** for anyone who actually watches.

---

## Every Inning Is Built One Hitter at a Time

The third inning does not arrive as a package. It arrives as:

1. Leadoff groundout — pitcher wins
2. Single — hitter wins
3. Strikeout — pitcher wins
4. Fly out — pitcher wins

Three wins, one loss. The inning might be **pitcher-favorable** even if that single led to trouble later.

Fans have always felt innings this way. *"He was good that inning except for the two-out hit."* PSIS just writes it down.

---

## Every Pitcher Wins or Loses Battles Differently

| Pitcher type | How he wins at-bats |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Power arm | Strikeouts, fly outs |
| Sinker guy | Ground balls, double plays |
| Craft lefty | Weak contact, sequencing |
| Reliever | One or two hitters, max intensity |

The box score might show similar lines for two very different nights. The **at-bat log** tells you who each pitcher actually was.

---

## What "Winning" an At-Bat Means (In Plain Language)

Without jargon:

| Pitcher wins | Hitter wins |
|--------------|-------------|
| Strikeout | Walk |
| Fly out | Hit |
| Ground out | |

When runs score on a hit or a bases-loaded walk, the damage matters more — the way any fan would tell the story: *"That single scored two."*

When the inning ends with runners on base who never scored, the pitcher **escaped** — the way any fan would nod: *"He got out of it."*

That is the whole philosophy. No doctorate required.

---

## Stories You Already Tell

You have said these things. So have we:

- *"He won that battle."*
- *"That was a cheap hit."*
- *"Best sequence of the night."*
- *"One bad inning."*

PSIS is a way to keep those sentences **honest and consistent** — so the pitcher hears the same story the fan saw.

---

## The Scorebook in the Third Row

Before computers, someone always kept score. Symbols for outs, hits, pitches. **Narrative in shorthand.**

PSIS is that tradition — updated so a coaching staff can share one book instead of twelve memories that disagree.

You do not have to use it to believe in the idea: **every at-bat tells a story, and stories are worth saving.**
